"Tell Me About Yourself"

This is the most common and important question. Answer it by discussing your:

  • Present: What do you do now? (School, major, grad year)
  • Past: What have you done? (Work, volunteer, relevant experiences)
  • Future: What do you want to do, and how does this role help? (Be specific)

"Tell me about a time when you set a goal for yourself. How did you achieve it?"

This is a 'behavioral' question, and there's a specific way to answer it! Describe the:

  • Situation: What was the situation? Set the scene
  • Task: What was the task at hand? What did you need to do?
  • Action: What actions did you take? What did you actually do?
  • Result: As a result, what happened? What did you learn from the experience?
  • Relevant: Share how the actions and results are relevant to the position you're interviewing for.

üß† Psych Tip of the Week: Future Me

Did you know your brain thinks of your future self the same way it thinks of a complete stranger?

Anything that feels "far" in time, space, or even social 'closeness' feels more abstract. It's hard to work so that an abstract "stranger" gets to benefit months from now.

There are ways to reduce this psychological distance.
In a blank document:

  1. Write 3 sentences from your future self, to your current self describing the feeling of having landed your new role. Be as specific as possible.
    • "I'm a few months into my job as a [Job Title] at [Company]..."
    • "That feeling of relief when I got the offer was incredible..."
    • "Thank you for not giving up. The hard work you're doing right now is what made this possible."

This closes the distance between you and your future. It makes the reward feel real and personal, giving you a powerful reason to work hard for that person‚Äîbecause that person is you.
